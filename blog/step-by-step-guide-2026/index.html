How to Get a Lithuania CASP License in 2026: Complete MiCA Guide

If you're a crypto founder evaluating Lithuania for an EU-passport CASP authorization under MiCA, this guide walks through the full setup — from the strategy decision through Bank of Lithuania review — based on real engagements we ran in 2025 and early 2026. By the end you'll know whether Lithuania CASP fits, what it costs, what to prepare, and what trips most founders up.

1. First decide: is Lithuania CASP actually the right authorization?

Lithuania CASP is the most expensive of the major crypto licensing options ($50K–$120K + €125K capital), so it has to be worth it. About 30% of inquiries that come in for "Lithuania VASP" actually need a different jurisdiction.

Lithuania CASP is the right fit if:

  • You target EU retail customers — MiCA passport across 27 member states is the killer feature.
  • You need a regulated badge for trust — Bank of Lithuania carries credibility EU and globally.
  • You can commit €125,000+ paid-up capital and accept a 4–8 month timeline.
  • You're an existing Lithuanian VASP needing to transition to CASP before 30 June 2026 grandfathering ends.
  • You're moving from Estonia VASP after the 2022–2024 Estonian crackdown.

Lithuania CASP is the wrong fit if:

  • You're bootstrapped — €125K capital + $50K+ setup is prohibitive. Consider Canada MSB ($8K–$25K) for fast launch.
  • You only serve non-EU customers — you're paying for a passport you won't use.
  • You want offshore tax efficiency — Lithuania has standard EU corporate tax (15%, or 5% small entity).
  • You need to launch in under 4 months — Bank of Lithuania review alone is 20-25 weeks.

2. Understand the MiCA timeline — why 30 June 2026 matters

MiCA enforcement began on 30 December 2024. Under the grandfathering provisions, pre-MiCA Lithuanian VASPs registered before 30 December 2024 can continue operating until 30 June 2026 — the 18-month grandfathering window. After that date, all Lithuanian crypto businesses must hold CASP authorization or cease operations.

For new applications since 30 December 2024, only CASP authorization is available — the pre-MiCA VASP regime is closed. So in 2026, "Lithuania VASP license" effectively means "Lithuania CASP license" except for existing VASPs in transition.

If you're an existing Lithuanian VASP: You should already be in transition. CASP authorization takes 4–8 months. Starting now (May 2026) means a tight finish before 30 June. Starting later means risk of operating gap.

3. Choose your CASP service class

MiCA defines three CASP classes by service scope and capital requirement. Choose carefully — Bank of Lithuania scrutinizes any mismatch between requested class and actual planned activities.

Most crypto exchanges target Class 2. If you also operate an order-book trading platform, Class 3. Class 1 is rarely the right answer for operating crypto businesses — it's restrictive (no custody, no exchange, no execution).

4. Form the Lithuanian UAB — the corporate vehicle

Lithuania CASP requires a Lithuanian UAB (Uždaroji Akcinė Bendrovė — private limited company). Foreign entities cannot directly hold a Lithuanian CASP authorization. Foreign-headquartered groups structure as: Foreign Parent → Lithuanian UAB (CASP).

UAB formation:

  • €2,500 minimum share capital — separate from the CASP capital requirement.
  • Notary execution — can be done remotely with apostilled signatures.
  • Registration with Centre of Registers — 5–10 business days.
  • Registered office — physical address in Lithuania (provided by your incorporation provider).

Total UAB formation timeline: 1–2 weeks. Cost: €2,500–€8,000.

5. Deposit the CASP capital — €50K–€150K

Once the UAB is formed, open a Lithuanian bank account and deposit the CASP class capital (€50K, €125K, or €150K). The capital is NOT consumed — it remains the company's working capital — but must be confirmed by an auditor before submitting the CASP application.

Banking is the bottleneck. Lithuanian banks have tightened KYC for crypto-adjacent businesses; opening a bank account for a new UAB intended to become a CASP can take 2–4 weeks. Crypto-friendly EMIs (Paysera, Verse, ConnectPay, Genome) are faster — 1–2 weeks. Many founders open accounts at both a traditional bank AND an EMI for redundancy.

6. Build the application package — what Bank of Lithuania actually reviews

The CASP application package is what Bank of Lithuania actually reviews. Quality matters enormously. Expect 200–500 pages, 30+ documents.

The core deliverables:

  1. Business plan + 3-year financial projections — Bank of Lithuania wants to see a credible commercial model with realistic customer acquisition, revenue, and cost projections. Generic projections fail.
  2. AML/KYC compliance program — 5AMLD/6AMLD compliant. 60–120 pages. Risk assessment specific to YOUR business.
  3. IT and security framework — cybersecurity, custody segregation, business continuity, outsourcing. 20–60 pages.
  4. Service descriptions — detailed description of each crypto-asset service, including target customers, transaction flows, fees.
  5. Customer-facing terms — terms of service, white papers (where applicable), risk disclosures, complaints procedure.
  6. Conflicts of interest policy — documented identification and management of conflicts.
  7. Outsourcing arrangements — written contracts with all critical service providers (cloud hosting, KYC vendors, transaction monitoring).

7. Fit-and-proper for key personnel — the most-failed step

Bank of Lithuania assesses every key person before authorization. Failures here are the most common cause of CASP rejection.

Each key person submits a fit-and-proper questionnaire (~15-20 pages) plus:

  • Detailed CV (Bank of Lithuania expects 5+ years for board members; 3+ years for MLRO)
  • Criminal record check from country of residence (apostilled, current within 3 months)
  • 2 professional references on letterhead
  • Photo ID
  • Disclosure of all directorships, shareholdings, regulatory history
  • Time-commitment declaration
  • Conflicts of interest declaration

Critical: Bank of Lithuania expects meaningful local substance — typically at least one board member or senior officer based in Lithuania, plus a local MLRO. Purely foreign management triggers substance concerns. Plan for €20K–€80K/year in Lithuanian-resident MLRO + at least one substantive Lithuanian board member or operations head.

8. Submit the application + survive the 20–25 week review

Once the package is complete, submit via the Bank of Lithuania supervisory portal with the €5,000–€10,000 application fee. Acknowledgement within 5 business days.

Bank of Lithuania review process under MiCA Article 62:

  1. Completeness check (3 months): Bank of Lithuania confirms the application is complete. If incomplete, requests additional information with response deadlines (RFIs).
  2. Substantive review (extended): Detailed review of business plan, AML program, IT framework, fit-and-proper, capital adequacy. May involve interviews with key personnel.
  3. Decision: Authorize, conditionally authorize, or refuse.

Total review: 20–25 weeks. Use the wait period actively — onboard MLRO, finalize IT systems, build customer pipeline, prepare marketing.

9. Common rejection reasons — what causes Bank of Lithuania to refuse

From real engagements:

  • Generic AML program — Bank of Lithuania reviewers spot generic templates immediately. Risk assessment must be business-specific.
  • Insufficient local substance — purely foreign management with no Lithuanian presence triggers substance concerns.
  • Key personnel without sufficient experience — board members with no financial-services background fail fit-and-proper.
  • Unrealistic financial projections — hockey-stick growth projections without justification trigger commercial credibility concerns.
  • Capital concerns — capital deposit funded from suspicious sources, or capital that doesn't survive the review period.
  • Inconsistent documentation — service descriptions inconsistent with business plan inconsistent with AML program. Bank of Lithuania checks consistency carefully.
  • Slow RFI responses — repeatedly missing RFI response deadlines suggests the applicant isn't taking the regulator seriously.

10. After authorization — EU passport + ongoing compliance

Once authorized, you're added to the public Bank of Lithuania CASP register and ESMA's central CASP register. Now:

EU passport activation:

  • To passport into another EU member state, file notification with Bank of Lithuania specifying receiving country and services.
  • Bank of Lithuania forwards to receiving competent authority within 1 month.
  • 30-day waiting period after receipt by receiving authority.
  • Then you can provide services in that country.
  • Each member state requires separate notification, but the underlying authorization is one Lithuanian CASP.

Ongoing compliance:

  • Bank of Lithuania annual supervision fee (€2,000–€8,000 volume-based)
  • Annual audited financials
  • Independent AML program review (annual or biennial)
  • Quarterly transaction reporting
  • Notify Bank of Lithuania of material changes (new services, new key personnel, capital changes)
  • Travel Rule compliance (FATF Recommendation 16)
  • DAC8 tax reporting (effective 2026)

FAQ

How long does it take to get a Lithuania CASP license in 2026?

Lithuania CASP authorization takes 4–8 months end-to-end. Pre-submission stages take 2–3 months (UAB formation, capital deposit, AML program build, application package). Bank of Lithuania review under MiCA Article 62 takes 20–25 weeks. Class 1 is faster (~4–5 months); Class 3 is slower (7–9 months).

Is Lithuania CASP worth €125K capital + $50K+ setup in 2026?

Yes, IF you target EU retail customers. The MiCA passport across 27 EU member states is unique to EU-authorized CASPs — Panama and Canada MSB don't provide it. For EU-focused crypto exchanges, custodians, and brokers, Lithuania CASP is the most cost-effective EU-regulated option (cheaper than Germany, France, Ireland, or Malta). For non-EU operators, the cheaper jurisdictions (Panama $15K–$45K, Canada $8K–$25K) usually win.

What's the biggest pitfall in Lithuania CASP setups?

Two pitfalls dominate: (1) insufficient fit-and-proper documentation — most common cause of RFI cycles. Get apostilled criminal record checks in week 1, not week 8; (2) insufficient local substance — Bank of Lithuania expects meaningful Lithuanian presence. Plan for a Lithuanian-resident MLRO and at least one substantive Lithuanian board member or operations head. Purely foreign management is a red flag.
